First off, I'll admit that I love the movie. I grew up with it. (I'm talking about the Gene Wilder version, of course.)I'll even admit to liking the movie better than the book. Which is something that doesn't happen very often with me.

That said, the book is really, really good. It held my four-year old's attention. It's silly, and it's fun.

And it's DARK.

For those of you who haven't read the book, let me underline this fact for you. Dahl takes pains to really detail the fact that Charlie and his family aren't just hungry and poor. They're destitute. Charlie sleeps on a mattress on the floor. In the winter they are cold, and they're starving to death.

And if you think I'm exaggerating on that last point, I'm not. One of the chapters is titled: The Family Begins to Starve.

But you know what? I like this book better because of that. It's not sanitized pablum written by committee to be inoffensive. It's the story of a little boy who is in a fucking awful situation, but he is still good and kind and polite and then something really nice happens to him.

That's a trope I can get behind.

Its it a good book to read with your kids? Absolutely.

That said, allow me to tangent off and share my thoughts as a total bastard:

If Willie Wonka actually hired workers and paid them a living wage, maybe Charlie Bucket wouldn't be starving to death in the first place.

Follow me here. Wonka is effectively running a company where everyone is paid in scrip. The Oompa Loompas are paid, quite literally, in beans. Beans that I'm guessing he has the Oompa Loompas themselves growing in some huge underground cavern.

Let's not even get into the ethical tarpit of the fact that Wonka uproots an entire indigenous culture and enslaves them. Let's just look at this from a raw numbers point of view. Pure economics.

The Oompa Loompas work in the factory. They are not paid. They never leave the factory. That means they don't pay rent. They don't buy groceries. They don't go to the movies, or take taxis ,or buy clothes.

But *everyone* buys Wonka's chocolate.

That means that money goes into the factory, but it doesn't come back out into the town.

As a result, the local economy is crap. And it's because of this that Charlie's dad can't get a decent job. What's more, it's because of this that his dad *loses* his shitty job, and his family is starving to death.

Willie Wonka isn't a childlike magic maker. He's a billionaire corporate fuckwit. He's the candy equivalent of Monsanto. There's no government oversight there. Osha would never have approved that bullshit boiled sweet boat and chocolate river. No. Dude is untouchable.

And don't tell me he isn't. That shit that goes on with the other kids? Nobody even *thinks* of suing him. None of the parents even *hint* at it. He probably owns half the judges in the state, and a handful of senators, too.

He's a fucking supervillian. And I would paid serious money to see a story where Batman kicks his ass.

*End Rant*

In closing, let me share something that Oot said while I was reading him this book:

"Dad, Willie Wonka is just a regular human, but he *is* a little bit of a wizard like you."

My feelings towards this book were lukewarm, that is to say, I didn't hate it, but I wasn't also particularly raving about it. To be more candid, I wouldn't have picked this book up if it didn't win the polls for the December face-to-face discussion of the book club.

Charlie and The Chocolate Factory is about, well, little Charlie Bucket and a chocolate factory owned by the creepy and weird Willy Wonka. Charlie's family is destitute as destitute can be, and poor little Charlie can only eat chocolate once a year - on his birthday. It didn't help that Willy Wonka's chocolate factory is just a stone's throw away from Charlie's family's house, and the sweet aroma of chocolate would torment and assail Charlie's senses each day.

One day, Willy Wonka announced that he would be giving away Golden Tickets to five children - just five  - to whom he will give a grand tour of the factory, which he has kept closely guarded for quite a time. The Golden Tickets can be found in Wonka's chocolate bars, and so the hunt for them began as kids gobbled up the candy left and right. Oh, but poor Charlie! Will he be able to get a Golden Ticket, with his family's poverty?

Such is the premise of this children's classic story supposedly replete with lessons for children and parents alike. I appreciate the part where kids and adults can learn a lesson or two from reading the book, but the fact that the lessons were imparted by a spooky, sarcastic and odd individual - Willy Wonka - didn't quite sit well with me. As we in the legal practice would put it: the witness is not credible, hence, his testimony should not be given credence. I feel exactly that way towards Willy Wonka. He wasn't a credible enough individual to tell off the parents (You're raising your children wrong!) and their children (TV is bad! Chewing gum is bad!); neither did I approve of the manner to which he resorted in conveying his (misplaced) opinions.

But that's just me. As a children's book, Charlie and The Chocolate Factory actually has some value. It's a challenge to the imagination, reading about those rooms full of chocolate inventions and candy concoctions. Movie outfits wouldn't have deemed it worthy of adaptations if the storyline did not guarantee colorful scenes and lots of singing, c/o the Oompa Loompas working in the factory. The book has its appeal to the young readers, I concede, but they were lost on me. Perhaps it's no longer age appropriate for me, yes?

Willy Wonka, an eccentric, slightly sinister but lovable genius, sends out 5 golden tickets, in hopes of finding an heir for his beloved Chocolate Factory. They end up in the hands of 4 horrible little brats: Augustus Gloop. A gluttonus pig, never without a chocolate bar.
Violet Beauregard. A competive girl, with a mean streak.
Veruca Salt. A snobby spoiled little brat, constantly demanding things.
Mike Teevee. A TV obsessed obnoxious child, who loves violence.
And of course one poor, but excessively sweet boy named Charlie. In other words, our little hero.
The children and their dopey parents enter the fabulous, famous Chocolate Factory and in true Dahl fashion, find out that their surroundings might not be that sweet, after all.

Now, what I like about this book.
Willy Wonka. He can be a bit annoying, from memory. But he's lively, highly energetic (as in a constant sugar high) and a pretty charming character. I did get the sense of maybe an underlying insanity or evilness in him, but it's a Roald Dahl book. I'd expect nothing less.

The Oompa Loompas. Racist allegations notwithstanding. These lovable scamps show off Dhal's mischievous side. Always there to lend a helping hand and offer an informative but gruesome poem. I can only describe them as delightfully frightening. And with 3 versions of them, you are pretty spoiled for choice.
I admit, I was creeped out by the orange ones as a kid and much prefer the book and the Tim Burton versions.

The Oompa Loompa poems. I think Dahl is at his best when he's writing dark material and these poems prove that. I know many people adore the '71 Oompa Loompa songs and they are cute, charming and creative. But I'm sorry, give me the poems any day. (Well, okay. The TV rant/poem is a bit drawn out, but I still prefer them.)
Without being patronizing, Dahl is able to teach children morals with some vivid gruesome imagery, laced with typical Dahl nonsense but with quite a bit of bite. Enough so that your child might just think twice about having that piece of gum. (Why is chewing gum so bad, anyway?)

The Factory and the inventions.
I think neither film version got this 100% accurate. The first was a bit too behind to really capture the factory in all it's glory, the second one does do well to expand upon the old film version factory and captures that demented but colorful factory quite well. But Roald Dahl's imagination has yet to be beaten.
His factory almost leaps off the page and is very vivid and creative.
The factory is seemingly endless. It's a bit twisted from what I can remember and has a sort of almost ominous feel, throughout the tour, simply because it seems a little bit unpredictable. You could end up in a fantastic happy room, filled with delight, or chance upon a room that might be a little more scary than the brats' downfalls.
The inventions are all silly nonsense, sure to have the kids laughing at the absurdity of Willy Wonka. Of course, they are given silly little names or just do silly things.

The cast of characters.
Every character, in usual Dahl style, is over-exaggerated and hilarious. The brats (who are supposed to be the 7 Deadly Sins?) are all putrid little so and sos. Each and every time they get their comeuppance, someone is likely to cheer.

Of course, Charlie's dad makes an appearance as does his Grandparents. Each charming in their own little way. (If I remember correctly.)

The story.
Roald Dahl gives us another delightfully macabre Fairy Tale, whilst a bit moralizing, is still an engaging read for children. I can't really fault it, I mean what kid doesn't want to own a chocolate factory? Especially the one Dahl has created.

What I don't like.

Charlie Bucket. He's such a little goody two shoes. (Mind you, I'm not too fond of any version of Charlie. But that's just me.) I acknowledge that he is a pretty damn good role model and he's actually not that unrealistic. A lot of poor children really are very unselfish and very kind. Hell, some children in general are really like that. I know some and they're not as bland as Charlie would lead you to believe. But even as a child I didn't like him much. Then again, I was always in detention, so I suppose I should have taken more notice of his advice lol. Thankfully, he's just sort of there and the weird and wonderful stuff around him distracts you from the dullness of Charlie. So he's not a terrible drawback and I'm sure younger children don't notice him that much, either.

The edits. Now, as a child I didn't notice this. But this book has been through quite a few editions by now. As a 90's kid, I got the hacked at but now "finished" product. It's probably not a big thing and many people probably won't even notice it, but it does seem a little bit on the incomplete and unstructured side. Even for a Dahl novel. So it's just a little nitpick.

The moralizing. Roald Dahl, God love him, loves to judge people. Especially parents. I can sort of agree with him on some aspects. For example, I think he's right to judge the hell out of Matilda's parents.
This book is just a huge critique on what he views as horrible aspects of children in his society. He may be right on quite a few things, TV does desensitize some children and can lead to obnoxiousness (not always, but still.) But I think Violet Beauregard is just his personal pet peeve. Yeah, kids chewing gum can be annoying, but damn, a bit overboard with his revenge here. XD
This book does judge some people for their parenting style and while he may be right on a few occasions, seems to be a little bit generalizing. It still fits with his Kids power theme, so I guess it's not altogether a bad thing. The one thing I thought didn't fit this theme was Charlie's message. A child who does everything that he's told is our role model. It does seem a little bit out of place, when children in Dahl novels are usually there to correct the adults. He is a good role model, but I do take issue with this blind obedience message. It's not always the best.
Again, I think this is me over analyzing and nitpicking. At the end of the day, it's just my personal take on Charlie. I do like the family is important message, though.

All in all, this is still a good book by Dahl. Not my personal favorite and I don't think it's his best work. But I can certainly see it's appeal. It's a good story with some good morals, proving that books can be just as entertaining, if not more so, than TV, provided you give them a chance.
